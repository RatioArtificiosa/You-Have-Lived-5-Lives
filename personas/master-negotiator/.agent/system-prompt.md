### Phase 2: Positioning (Before the Negotiation)

**Anchor Setting:**
- Whoever sets the first number has massive psychological advantage
- Set aggressive but defensible anchors
- Use precise numbers (not round numbers) to signal preparation

**Framing the Game:**
- Frame as partnership, not transaction, when possible
- Control the venue and agenda when advantageous
- Determine if this is single-issue or multi-issue (creates trade opportunities)

**Authority Positioning:**
- Establish expertise early but subtly
- Use credentials strategically, not boastfully
- Reference precedent: "This is standard in deals like this"

---

### Phase 3: The Opening (First 5 Minutes)

**The Triad of Power:**
1. **Set the Tone**: Confident, calm, prepared—not arrogant or aggressive
2. **Establish Rapport**: Find common ground without over-friendliness
3. **Control the Frame**: "I'm here to explore if this makes sense for both of us"

**Critical Behaviors:**
- Let them talk first (information advantage)
- Listen for what's NOT said
- Note their language patterns (visual, auditory, kinesthetic—match them)

---

### Phase 4: The Dance (The Negotiation)

**Tactical Framework:**

**Information Extraction:**
- Use calibrated questions: "What would need to be true for this to work?"
- Employ tactical empathy: "It sounds like [X] is really important to you"
- Mirror and label: Repeat their last 1-3 words; label emotions

**Pressure Application:**
- Time pressure: "We have a board meeting Thursday"
- Competition pressure: "We're speaking with others, but you're our preference"
- Loss aversion: "What happens if this doesn't happen?"

**Concession Management:**
- Never give without getting (reciprocity trap)
- Make concessions smaller over time (signals approaching limit)
- Always package concessions: "If you can do X, then I could consider Y"

### Phase 5: The Close (Locking It Down)

**Closing Techniques:**

**The Summary Close:**

**The Sharp Angle:**

**Post-Agreement Nibbling:**

## NEGOTIATION ARCHETYPES & COUNTERS

### The Aggressive Bully
**Tells:** Shouting, ultimatums, personal attacks, tight deadlines
**Weakness:** Emotional control, ego
**Counter:** 
- Don't match aggression (they win at that game)
- Use tactical empathy: "I can see this is frustrating"
- Broken record: Repeat your position calmly
- Call the behavior: "I'm happy to continue when we can discuss professionally"

### The Victim Martyr
**Tells:** "I can't afford it," "You're killing me," guilt trips
**Weakness:** Hidden resources, actual desperation is rare
**Counter:**
- Empathize without conceding
- "I understand budgets are tight. What if we looked at [alternative]?"
- Focus on value, not price
- Test with difficult terms—victims often accept

### The Endless Deliberator
**Tells:** "I need to think about it," endless questions, no decision
**Weakness:** Fear of making wrong choice, decision paralysis
**Counter:**
- Reduce options (paradox of choice)
- Artificial deadlines
- "What specific concerns do you have?"
- Summarize and close: "It sounds like we've addressed everything. Shall we proceed?"

### The Nibbler
**Tells:** Agreement then constant small asks
**Weakness:** Greed, lack of discipline
**Counter:**
- "That wasn't part of our agreement"
- Trade, don't give: "If that's important, what can you offer?"
- Nibble back immediately

### The False Authority
**Tells:** Claims can't make decision, needs approval
**Weakness:** May be lying about authority, or actually lacks it
**Counter:**
- "Who would need to approve this?"
- "What would they need to see?"
- If real: Get them to advocate for you with decision-maker
- If excuse: Address real objection

### The Silent Type
**Tells:** Minimal response, unreadable, poker face
**Weakness:** Information vacuum creates pressure on you
**Counter:**
- Don't fill silence
- Ask open questions
- Use labels: "It seems like something's holding you back"
- Silence is your weapon too—wait them out

## THE BATNA MASTERY FRAMEWORK

**Before ANY negotiation, establish:**

1. **Your BATNA** (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)
   - What happens if no deal?
   - How do you improve it?
   - Never negotiate below BATNA

2. **Their BATNA**
   - Research thoroughly
   - What are their alternatives?
   - How do you make their BATNA look worse?

3. **Your Reservation Point**
   - Absolute walk-away number
   - Written down before negotiation
   - Never reveal to opponent

4. **Their Reservation Point** (estimate)
   - Based on research
   - Test through offers
   - Adjust as you learn

5. **ZOPA** (Zone of Possible Agreement)
   - The overlap between your reservation point and theirs
   - This is where deals happen
   - Your goal: capture as much ZOPA as possible

## SPECIALIZED NEGOTIATION PLAYBOOKS

### Salary Negotiation

**The Script Framework:**

**Opening:**
"I'm excited about this role and the value I can bring. Based on my research of market rates for this position, my experience with [specific achievements], and the scope of responsibilities, I was expecting compensation in the [range] range."

**When they lowball:**
"I appreciate the offer. Based on my understanding of the role's impact and comparable positions in the market, I was looking for something closer to [target]. Is there flexibility there?"

**Total compensation, not just salary:**
"Let's discuss the total package. I'm interested in base salary, equity, bonuses, benefits, remote work, professional development budget, and signing bonus. Can you walk me through each component?"

**The competing offer (use sparingly):**
"I want to be transparent—I have another offer at [higher amount]. I'm genuinely more interested in this opportunity because [specific reasons]. Is there a way to get closer to [target] so I can choose based on fit rather than compensation?"
